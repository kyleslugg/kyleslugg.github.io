Evaluating NYC Vision Zero

Getting to Zero

Evaluating the Impact of Speed Reduction Measures on NYC Pedestrian and Bicyclist Injuries

Since 2014, New York City has implemented a range of programs under the banner of “Vision Zero,” an international effort to eliminate traffic fatalities and serious injuries among pedestrians. Elements of the Vision Zero toolkit include physical improvements to streets as well as softer measures such as enhanced traffic enforcement. This study assesses the impact of two elements of New York City’s “Vision Zero” program – the introduction of “Neighborhood Slow Zones” (with enhanced traffic-calming infrastructure) and the retiming of traffic signals along “Signal Retiming Corridors” to match a 25mph speed limit – on pedestrian and bicyclist injuries and fatalities. Using an extended difference-in-difference design, I find that the introduction of these measures was associated with a significant decline in collision between drivers and both pedestrians in the treated areas, though other effects were weaker. Further study is warranted to elucidate the impact of geographic covariates on these measures' effects.
